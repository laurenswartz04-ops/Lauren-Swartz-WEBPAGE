ENGR 11A

These are my projects revolving around CAM and CAD. I hope to explore designing and ideating to create physical creations.

Nametag

My first project has three parts, exploring the capacity and effectiveness of 3D printers and designs. To start out, I printed a simple nametag, familiarizing myself with how to import designs into the Prusa Slicer software, alter them to my preferences, and transfer them to the Prusa MINI+ to print. I edited the words to display my first and last name, and selected the writing font that I preferred. After exporting the file design to a flashdrive, I set up my first filament color. It began to print, and when the color change was required, I switched out the filament.

My finished nametag printed fairly well, with strings around the lettering. I believe this could be caused by the font choice being a bit thin and the temperature. The font size also varies, with some sections of lettering appearing much thinner or thicker than others. These inconsistencies may be due to difficulty of my font size choice.

Calibration Print

For my calibration (torture) print to test the capabilities of the 3D printer, I chose to print a DNA double helix. This showed me that the printer is able to produce tall, stable, unsupported prints with repetitive complex designs and generally effective bridging between different components of the print. There was some slight stringyness and draping of filament between some of the rungs(or bridges) of the double helix, but those could be easily removed. These minute errors could be a result of incorrect retracting speed, but also potentially due to lack of supports. I learned that thicker unsupported pieces of a print are less difficult to produce, as compared to thinner unsupported pieces. Also, the slight overhangs of the curved sides were successful, showing a strength of the print. The print had a smooth, stable, base that allows it to stand up straight. My biochemistry friend who I gave this to was a big fan, and it now stands on his desk. Overall, I was happy with this print.

Open-source Assistive Device

For my open-source assistive print of the project, I made a jar lid opener for users that may struggle with hand strength and dexterity. It could also be for people who experience joint pain. I found the design on the website MakersMakingChange.com. The print came out almost entirely successful. The only issue that I came across was that the print struggled from poor bridging on the end of the hook. This is something I was initially slightly concerned with, but the hook did not seem to be too large of an unsupported protrusion. The preview on the Prusa software did acknowledge it as bridge infill, but this draping of filament is most likely due to speed of extrusion of filament or lack of supports for the cantilever. I could easily clean it up to be a fully working jar opener, so I wouldn't make any changes!
